Likely Questions & Answers

Q: What happened to the 26% that weren’t covered?
Of the roughly 800 uncovered classes YTD: ~80% were “couldn’t find a sub” capacity failures, ~14% were families who’d opted out of subs (“NO SUBS Students”), and ~5% were too late to find one. So the headline is that the gap is mostly a capacity problem we can work on, not families turning down coverage.
Q: What percent of families don’t want a sub class?
Only about 4% of all sub requests this year were “NO SUBS” families — 118 classes out of 3,004. It’s a small but real preference signal we honor, and it’s steady, not growing.
Q: Is 74% trending up or down? How does it compare to last year?
74% is our YTD number. We don’t have a strong year-over-year baseline because the volume of disruption has grown alongside our active student base. What I really track is the absolute number of classes we save — over 2,200 this year — and the pattern of failures, which is what tells us where to focus.
Q: What’s driving 3,000 sub requests — is volume going up?
Yes, volume scales with our student base. The more important measure for us is sub requests per active class, which is roughly steady. If that ever spikes, that’s the signal that something has shifted in instructor stability.
Q: Why was the week of May 25 such a big week? How many instructors were out?
That was Memorial Day week, which is always our biggest PTO concentration of the year. 29 instructors were out that week — close to one in five of the team — which generated 270 class disruptions in five days. The team rescheduled 165 of those as makeups and covered the rest with subs, which is why that week is the proof point I keep coming back to.
Q: What did the 165-makeup week cost us?
Weeks like that one cost more in emergency-sub premium pay than a normal week — that’s exactly the kind of cost we want to bring down as we refine the find-a-sub workflow and the Scheduling Bots. The recruiting and capacity work I’ll talk about next is part of that.
Q: What does “covered” mean exactly?
Covered means a sub instructor was found and the class went on as scheduled. It does not include classes that were rescheduled as makeups — those are tracked separately.
Q: How are you measuring against any benchmark?
There isn’t a clean industry benchmark for this. The honest answer is we’re benchmarking against ourselves — watching the coverage rate, the per-student request rate, and the failure mix — and we use the failure mix to decide where to invest, whether that’s recruiting, scheduling automation, or workflow.
